Two-Part Names: How Scientists Name Living Things

📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why does every living thing have a scientific name built from old words?
Scientists give every living thing a two-part scientific name, usually built from Latin (and sometimes Greek) words, so that scientists anywhere in the world — no matter what language they speak day to day — can agree on exactly which plant or animal they mean. This system was created by a scientist named Carl Linnaeus in the 1700s, and it is still used today.

🔬 PART 2 · READING: ONE NAME FOR EVERY LIVING THING
In the 1700s, a scientist named Carl Linnaeus set up a system so that every living thing would have a scientific name made of two parts, usually built from Latin and sometimes Greek. That way, scientists anywhere in the world could agree on exactly which plant or animal they meant, no matter what language they spoke at home. Human beings are called Homo sapiens, Latin for “wise human.” The famous dinosaur Tyrannosaurus rex mixes Greek word-parts meaning “tyrant lizard” with the Latin word rex, “king” — together, “tyrant lizard king.” Even the gray wolf has a two-part name, Canis lupus, simply the Latin words for “dog” and “wolf.”

🤗 NEED HELP?
If a word-part feels tricky, reread the passage above and look for that exact word. Remember: most of these word-parts are Latin, but tyranno- and -saurus are Greek — scientific names sometimes mix both languages.
