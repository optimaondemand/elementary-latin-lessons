📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
How do you run a city without a king?
For part of its history, ancient Rome was not ruled by a single king — it was a rēspūblica, a "public thing" that belonged to the people. Roman citizens gathered in the forum to talk, shop, hear news, and conduct business, while a group of leaders called the senātus met to help decide what was best for the city. Later, Rome became an empire ruled by emperors, but the words and ideas from its republic days are still part of how we talk about government today.

🏛️ PART 2 · READING: A CITY GOVERNED BY ITS PEOPLE
For part of its history, Rome was a rēspūblica — literally "the public thing" — the idea that the city and its decisions belonged to the people as a whole, not to one all-powerful king. Roman citizens gathered in the forum, the large open square at the center of the city, to talk, shop, hear news, and conduct business and government affairs. A group of Roman leaders called the senātus met there as well, discussing and deciding what was best for the city; Roman senators were recognized by the togas they wore. Rome later became an empire ruled by emperors, but the forum itself still stands in Rome today, and visitors can walk through its ruins.

🔍 PART 3 · DERIVATIVE HUNT

A derivative is an English word that grew out of a Latin one. Match each Latin word below to the English word it helped create.

⚡ TRY IT · MATCH THE DERIVATIVE
Which English word grew out of this Latin word?
1. senātus — "a group of Roman leaders who met to decide things for the city"
2. forum — "the open public square where Romans gathered to talk, shop, and conduct city business"
3. rēspūblica — literally "the public thing," the idea that the city belonged to the people, not one king
4. lēx — "law"
5. populus — "the people"
🤗 NEED HELP?
Some of these Latin roots look like more than one English word (populus gives us both popular and population). If a match feels tricky, focus on the meaning given next to the Latin word, not just how it sounds.
