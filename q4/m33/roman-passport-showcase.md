## Framing

This is the last week of Elementary Latin for the year, and this assignment is a celebration, not a test. It is the closest thing this course has to a summative project, but it should be presented and shared rather than graded for correctness. Students are not being asked to prove mastery of every fact from every module — they are being asked to look back with pride at a year of Roman words, Roman gods, Roman daily life, and Roman engineering, and to show someone else what they remember. Keep the tone warm throughout: this is a finish line worth celebrating, not a final exam to fear.

## The Assignment: Roman Passport Showcase

Across the year, students have kept a running "Roman Passport" (a simple booklet or Word document, by lane), adding one new entry each quarter:

- Quarter 1 Milestone (Module 8): Passport Page 1
- Quarter 2 Milestone (Module 16): Passport Page 2
- Quarter 3 Milestone (Module 24): Passport Page 3

This week, students add their fourth and final Roman Passport entry, matching the same format used in Quarters 1–3: one drawing, one fact, and one Latin word learned this quarter. Once that final entry is complete, students present and share their now-complete, full-year Roman Passport — four pages that trace an entire year's journey through the Roman world, from their very first Latin greeting to everything they've learned about Rome's daily life, engineering, and legacy.

There is no written test, quiz, or graded quick check this module. Session 2 of this final week is entirely showcase and celebration time.

## Foundations (K-2)

Foundations students share their Roman Passport aloud, with a grown-up or with the class. A simple structure works well: "Flip through your Passport page by page, and tell a grown-up or your class one thing you remember from each page." A student might name a Latin word, point to a drawing, or retell a favorite fact in their own words.

This sharing is completion-based and celebratory. There is nothing to grade beyond participation and completion — a student who opens their Passport and shares something from each page has fully succeeded at this assignment. There is no expectation of polished delivery, memorized facts, or correct terminology; the goal is simply for the student to take pride in showing what they made and remember.

## Primary (3-5)

Primary students share their completed Roman Passport the same way — page by page, aloud or in writing — and add one more piece: a short "here's what I learned this year" wrap-up, a few sentences long, written or spoken. This is not a formal essay and should not be treated as one. It might mention a favorite god, a fact about Roman daily life, or something about Roman roads or aqueducts that stuck with the student all year. The tone should be celebratory and personal, not analytical.

This assignment is completion-based. Teacher feedback should be light and encouraging — a note of appreciation or a follow-up question — rather than a numeric grade or rubric score. The measure of success is a finished four-page Passport and a genuine, if brief, reflection on the year, not correctness or writing polish.

## Throughout

However this is run in a given classroom or home, the spirit should stay the same: this is a year-end celebration honoring everything a student has learned, not a final exam. Keep the framing warm, keep the bar for success at "shared and complete," and let the Roman Passport itself — four pages built one quarter at a time — do the work of showing how far each student has come.
