📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why do "discipline" and "magistrate" sound like school words?
Latin school words are still part of English today. A magister was a Roman teacher, and the English word magistrate — a judge or official — grew out of it. A discipulus was a Roman student, and the English word discipline grew out of it too. Today you'll step inside a Roman school, called a lūdus, and see how different a school day there really was.

🏫 PART 2 · READING: A DAY AT THE LŪDUS
Roman children usually began school, called a lūdus, around age six or seven. A discipulus (student) practiced writing on a wax tablet with a pointed tool called a stylus, smoothing the wax flat again to reuse it — an ancient eraser. The magister (teacher) also taught arithmetic, sometimes with a counting tool called an abacus. Roman school discipline was much stricter than a modern classroom.
