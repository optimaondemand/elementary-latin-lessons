The Legiō: Rome's Organized Army
Lesson
📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
How do you keep thousands of people organized at once?
The Roman army was famous for two things: its organization and its engineering. Today you'll learn how a huge unit called a legiō stayed organized by splitting into smaller teams — and how the same soldiers who marched together also built roads, bridges, and camps everywhere they went.
🎯 BY THE END OF THIS LESSON YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
I can use two Latin words: legiō (a large, organized group of Roman soldiers) and mīles (a soldier).
2
I can explain why the Roman army divided a legiō into smaller organized units.
3
I can name two English words that grew out of these Latin words: legion and military.

🛡️ PART 2 · READING: AN ARMY BUILT ON ORDER
The largest organized unit in the Roman army was called a legiō, a group that could include several thousand mīles, or soldiers. A legiō was too large to move or work as one crowd, so it was divided into smaller units, each with its own job — much like a well-run team split into groups with different roles. Wherever the army traveled, its mīles built roads, bridges, walls, and camps, using the same order and skill that kept the legiō running. The group also carried a standard, a pole topped with a symbol such as an eagle, so every soldier could recognize their own unit and stay organized.
A legiō divided into neat, even rows — not a crowd.
