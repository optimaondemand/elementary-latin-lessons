🗺️ PART 2 · READING: THE ROMAN ROAD NETWORK
You may have heard the old saying, "all roads lead to Rome." It's a saying people have repeated for a very long time, though it isn't an exact quotation from any one ancient writer — it's simply an old expression that grew out of how well-connected Rome's roads really were. Roman engineers built their roads straight and paved them with stone, so an army, a messenger, or a trader could move quickly no matter the weather. Along the way, milestones marked the distance, so travelers always knew how far they had gone on their iter (journey). One of the oldest and most famous of these roads, the Via Appia (the Appian Way), was begun in 312 BC and helped connect Rome to the rest of Italy.
🛣️ A paved Roman via receding toward the hills, with a stone milestone marking the distance.

Roman builders were famous for their roads. They dug down, packed layers of stone and gravel, and topped them with flat stones so the road stayed dry, smooth, and strong for a very long time.

🧭 Did you know?People still say all roads lead to Rome. Many of the biggest Roman roads started at the city of Rome and spread out like the spokes of a wheel.
📏 Did you know?Roman roads were built as straight as possible. A straight road was the quickest way to march soldiers or carry messages from town to town.
🪨 Did you know?The roads were paved with heavy flat stones set on layers of gravel. Some Roman roads are so strong that people can still walk on them today, almost two thousand years later.
