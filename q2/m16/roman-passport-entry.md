## Winter Roman Festival (light, informational, non-religious)
This is a brief, fun cultural note — not a lesson requiring deep content, and not religious instruction.

**What to share with students:** In December, ancient Romans celebrated a festival called **Saturnalia**, named for the god Saturn. It was one of the most popular and eagerly-anticipated festivals of the whole Roman year. During Saturnalia, Romans feasted, gave each other small gifts, decorated their homes, and played games where the usual social rules were temporarily turned upside-down for fun (for example, some ordinary rules about who served whom were playfully reversed for the day).

**Framing note:** Keep this purely as a historical and cultural fact about ancient Rome — do not connect it to the meaning of any modern holiday. (Some later winter celebrations share surface customs like gift-giving and feasting, but historians do not agree on a direct line between them and Saturnalia, so that connection should be left out or, at most, mentioned only as an open historical question — never asserted.)

**Simple classroom-appropriate activity idea:** Students make a small Latin-word bookmark (writing one Latin word they've learned this quarter on a paper bookmark, decorated as they like) and exchange it with a classmate or family member as a "Saturnalia gift," OR the class holds a simple "Saturnalia Day" with a Roman-themed treat and a lighthearted game. Keep the whole activity short, fun, and squarely historical/cultural — a celebratory note to close the quarter, not a unit of study.
