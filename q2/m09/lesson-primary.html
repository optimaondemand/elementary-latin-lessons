🔢 PART 2 · READING: NUMERALS THAT NEVER RETIRED
Romans wrote their numbers using letters, not digits — I for one, V for five, X for ten, and now two new shapes: L for fifty and C for one hundred. We don't use Roman numerals for everyday math anymore, but they never fully disappeared. The modern Olympic Games are numbered this way, so the 2020 Tokyo Games appeared on programs as the Games of the XXXII Olympiad. Roman numerals also help us tell apart rulers who share a name, like Queen Elizabeth II or King Charles III.

▶  Go Further: a strange little number puzzle (optional)
Look closely at the words for 18 and 19: duodēvīgintī literally means "two from twenty," and ūndēvīgintī means "one from twenty." Instead of counting up from ten the way English does ("eighteen" = eight + ten), Latin sometimes counts backward from the next big number. The numeral shapes XVIII and XIX don't work quite the same way — but the spoken words are a fun little detour.

🤗 NEED HELP?
If a numeral feels tricky, break it into pieces: X is ten, V is five, and each I adds one. Ūndecim, duodecim, and the rest all start from the word for ten, the same way ūndecim ("one-ten," roughly) starts building past ten.
