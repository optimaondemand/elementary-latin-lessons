📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
How do you move water for miles with no pumps or motors?
Romans solved this with aqueducts: long stone channels engineered with an extremely slight, carefully measured downhill slope, so gravity alone could carry water from a distant spring or lake all the way into a city — feeding fountains, baths, and homes along the way.

🌉 PART 2 · READING: A BRIDGE FOR WATER
An aqueductus ("aqueduct") worked using gravity: engineers gave the water channel a very slight, carefully measured downward slope, so it could carry aqua ("water") for miles from a distant spring into a city without any pumps. Where the land dipped into a valley, Romans built tall stone arches — an arcade — to carry the channel across and keep that steady slope going. One famous example still standing today is the Pont du Gard in southern France, a spectacular multi-tiered stone aqueduct bridge.
