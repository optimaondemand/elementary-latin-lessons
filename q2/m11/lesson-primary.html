📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Octō means eight. So why is October the tenth month?
Every month name on your calendar started as a Latin word. Most of them make sense right away — but four of them hide a strange puzzle. Today you'll solve it, and it all comes down to how the Romans used to count their year.

📜 PART 2 · READING: THE OLD ROMAN YEAR
According to Roman tradition, the very earliest Roman calendar began the year in Mārtius (March), named for the god Mars, and had only ten months in all, ending in December. By that old count, the months we now call September, October, November, and December really were the 7th, 8th, 9th, and 10th months — which is exactly why they were named after the numbers septem (seven), octō (eight), novem (nine), and decem (ten). According to old Roman accounts, two more months — Iānuārius and Februārius — were later added at the start of the year, and eventually the new year shifted to January. The number-names stuck anyway, even though the months had all moved.
