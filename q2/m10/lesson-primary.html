📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why do some day names sound Roman and others don't?
Every day of your week traces back to something the Romans saw in the sky. But English didn't keep all seven Latin names — some got swapped out along the way. Today you'll become a word detective and figure out which days stayed Roman and which ones changed, and why.

3

🕵️ PART 2 · READING: A WEEK-LONG DETECTIVE STORY
The Romans named each day of the week for the sun, the moon, or one of five wandering stars they linked to their gods: dies Sōlis, dies Lūnae, dies Mārtis, dies Mercuriī, dies Iovis, dies Veneris, and dies Saturnī. English kept the pattern whole for three of those days — Sunday, Monday, and Saturday are still Sun's day, Moon's day, and Saturn's day. But English speakers were also shaped by old Germanic and Norse traditions, so Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, and Friday were renamed for the gods Tiw, Woden, Thor, and Frigg — gods that English speakers saw as matching Mars, Mercury, Jupiter, and Venus.
