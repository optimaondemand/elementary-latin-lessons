1Adding a Class Video (the 🎬 slot)

Every lesson includes a placeholder that reads "🎬 Facilitator video goes here." That slot is yours. It's the spot to add a short clip that connects the module's Roman-world topic to something your class will love — and to let your personality into the lesson. You do not have to fill it, but a well-chosen 2–3 minute video is one of the easiest ways to make Latin feel alive.

There are two ways to fill it, depending on your access:

(a) Easiest — no repo access needed (do this in Canvas)

The lesson students see is an embedded page inside a Canvas module page. Your video sits in the Canvas page around that embed, right above it. You never touch any code.

  1. In Canvas, open the lesson's module page.
  2. Click Edit (top right).
  3. Click in the space above the embedded lesson, in the Rich Content Editor.
  4. Add your video one of two ways:
    • Paste a YouTube or Vimeo link on its own line — Canvas usually auto-previews it; or
    • Use Insert → Media (or the media icon) to embed a clip directly.
  5. Click Save. Your video now appears each time a student opens that lesson.
Tip: Because the video lives on the Canvas page (not inside the lesson embed), you can change it any time, per class, without affecting anyone else's course. Different section? Different video. Totally fine.

(b) Permanent swap — for the repo maintainer

If you want the video baked into the lesson itself (so it travels with the shared lesson for every class), whoever maintains the GitHub lessons repository can replace the placeholder block's embed URL directly in the lesson HTML. This is a permanent, course-wide change — use it for a clip you're confident every section should see.

What makes a good pick?
  • Short — aim for 2–5 minutes. Little learners fade fast.
  • Age-appropriate — friendly narration, clean visuals, K–5 tone.
  • Ad-safe — preview it start to finish; watch for surprise ads or "up next" clutter. Consider a distraction-free player when possible.
  • Relevant — tie it to the module's Roman-world topic (a virtual tour of the Forum, how an aqueduct works, Roman numerals, a myth retelling, gladiator daily life, etc.).
  • Fresh & engaging — this is your place to bring in current, exciting, relevant material. A great new documentary short, a museum clip, a maker rebuilding a Roman gadget — if it makes kids lean in, use it.

2Living Latin Example Bank

Latin never really left — it's hiding in plain sight all around us. Use this bank for the "Guess Where It Came From" activity, as bell-ringers, or just to share a quick "did you know?" Each row is a genuine, verified connection.

Accuracy note: Every etymology below is a real Latin root — not a look-alike guess. Many science and space words that sound Latin are actually Greek (octopus, dinosaur, astronaut, telescope, planet). Where a familiar word has a Greek part mixed in, it's flagged so you can teach it honestly. When in doubt, check before you share — surface resemblance fools everyone.

3Using the Interactive Games

Every lesson comes with built-in, tap-and-drag games — students play right on the page:

  • Match — pair a Latin word with its meaning or picture.
  • Memory — flip-and-find card pairs.
  • Sort — drag items into the right group or category.
  • Guess — pick the meaning or the missing word.

Grades 3–5 lessons also offer an optional "beat your best time" timer — it's opt-in, encourages a friendly replay, and is never graded. Younger students (K–2) simply play at their own pace with no clock.

Guiding all of it is Magister Caesar, a friendly talking guide who explains, cheers, and nudges students along.

Nothing to grade. The games auto-check answers and are pure practice — there's no gradebook column and no submission to review. Let students replay freely; repetition is the point.

4The Two Lanes

The course runs in two parallel lanes so one classroom can serve the whole K–5 span. Assign each student the lane that fits — and reassign any time.

Grades K–2

Foundations

Gentle pacing, heavy on pictures, sounds, and simple matching. No timers. Built for pre-readers and early readers.

Grades 3–5

Primary

More reading, more vocabulary, the optional "beat your best time" timer, and richer Roman-world content.

  • Either lane can be reassigned per student — move a strong young reader up, or give an older student who's new to Latin a gentler start. It's your call, student by student.
  • Read-aloud and verbal responses are always OK for younger students. This is a built-in accommodation: if a K–2 learner (or anyone still building reading skills) does better speaking answers or having text read to them, that's fully acceptable and expected.
