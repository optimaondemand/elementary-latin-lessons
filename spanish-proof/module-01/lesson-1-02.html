LESSON 1.02 · THE MOST IMPORTANT SOUND LESSON OF THE YEAR
The 5 Spanish Vowels
Five sounds. Each one never changes. Learn these and you can read almost any Spanish word out loud.
🔥 TODAY'S BIG IDEA
Spanish has exactly five vowels — and each one makes the same sound every single time. No exceptions.

That one fact is the secret to reading Spanish. English vowels shift around constantly; Spanish vowels do not. Once you own these five sounds, you can look at a brand-new Spanish word and pronounce it correctly — even a word you have never seen before.

🎯 BY THE END OF THIS PAGE YOU WILL BE ABLE TO…
1
Make each of the five Spanish vowel sounds: a e i o u.
2
Explain why Spanish vowels are easier than English vowels.
3
Pronounce every vowel in a word — because Spanish has no silent vowels.
📚 PART 1 · WHY THIS MATTERS FOR AN ENGLISH SPEAKER

In English, one vowel letter can make many different sounds. Look at the letter A in these four words and say them out loud:

cat  ·  cake  ·  car  ·  about

Four different sounds — from one letter. English spelling doesn't tell you which one to use; you just know because you have heard the words a thousand times. Spanish does not work this way, and that is great news for you.

🇬🇧 English vowels: SHIFTING

The same letter changes sound depending on the word. You have to memorize each word's sound one at a time.

🇪🇸 Spanish vowels: FIXED

The letter A is always the "ah" in father. Every time. Learn the sound once, use it forever.

🔊 PART 2 · MEET THE FIVE — LISTEN AND REPEAT

Here is all of Spanish pronunciation, in five lines. Press play on each one and say it back out loud. Your brain will learn the rule, but your mouth needs to build the muscle.

A

like the ah in father — not the "ay" in cake

Examples: casa · mamá · para

E

like the e in bed, said a little shorter and cleaner

Examples: bebé · tres · tiene

I

like the ee in see — always

Examples: sí · fino · mi

O

like the oh in open, but shorter — don't let it drift

Examples: poco · solo · nosotros

U

like the oo in moon — always

Examples: tú · uno · fútbol

Now say all five in order, out loud, twice — a little faster the second time:

A E I O U
💡 PART 3 · THE ONE HABIT TO BREAK

In Spanish, you pronounce every vowel

English has silent letters — think of the silent e at the end of name or cake. Spanish does not do this. Every vowel you see gets its full sound.

Take the word estudiante (student). Your English brain wants to rush it and drop the last e. Don't. Say each vowel its own way:

e – s – t – u – d – i – a – n – t – e

Five vowels, all pronounced: e… u… i… a… e. This is the single hardest habit for English speakers — not the rolling R, not the double L. It's the vowels, because they feel almost like English but aren't. Slow down and give each one its clean, fixed sound.
