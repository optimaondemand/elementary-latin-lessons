¡Hola! Welcome to Spanish

👋 TODAY'S BIG IDEA
You are about to learn a language that 500 million people speak — and you already know more of it than you think.

This week is all about starting well: the sounds of Spanish and how to greet people. Today there is nothing to memorize. We are just going to look at why Spanish is worth your time, and you will say your very first word out loud.

📚 PART 1 · WHY SPANISH?

Spanish is the official language of 21 countries and the first language of more than 500 million people. In the United States it is the second most spoken language — and here in Florida you can hear it at the store, on the radio, in music, and in food. Learning even a little bit of it opens doors: to people, to places, and to being able to say "hello" to someone who was not expecting you to try.

Here is the good news for a first day: Spanish and English are cousins. They share thousands of words that look and mean almost the same thing. You are not starting from zero. You are starting from "already know some."

🌍 PART 2 · YOU ALREADY KNOW SOME SPANISH

Read these English words out loud: rodeo, plaza, patio, fiesta, taco, salsa. Every single one came into English straight from Spanish. You have been speaking a few Spanish words your whole life without noticing.

On Day 4 this week we will play a game with words like these — the Spanish hiding inside English. For now, just enjoy the head start.

🗣️ PART 3 · YOUR FIRST WORD

¡Hola! — Hello

Your first word of Spanish is ¡hola! It means hello, and it works with anyone — a friend, a teacher, a stranger. Notice the punctuation: Spanish puts an upside-down mark at the start and a normal one at the end.

  • Excitement or a greeting gets exclamation marks: ¡Hola!
  • A question gets question marks: ¿Cómo estás?

The upside-down ¡ and ¿ are a kindness to the reader — they tell you a sentence is going to be excited or a question before you start reading it. Press play and say it back out loud:

  • ¡Hola! Hello!

Say it out loud, not just in your head. Spanish lives in your mouth, not on the page.

★ A WORD ON COURTESY

Greeting someone in their own language is a small act of courtesy — it says "I see you, and I made the effort." That is a big part of what this course is really about: not just words, but how we treat the people who speak them.
