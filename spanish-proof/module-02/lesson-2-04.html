🌎 Spanish in Your World

Two languages, one big shared toolbox

Here's why Spanish keeps feeling familiar. Spanish grew directly out of Latin, the language of ancient Rome. English took a different path — but over the centuries it borrowed enormous numbers of Latin-based words too (many through French). So English and Spanish ended up sharing thousands of word-cousins that look alike and mean the same thing.

Words like these are called cognates (from Latin cognatus, "related by birth" — a cognate of the English word "kin"). When you spot a cognate, you get its meaning for free — no memorizing required.

Read these Spanish cognates. You already know every meaning — and each one is still color-coded by gender:

la familia — the family
la música — the music
la persona — the person
el animal — the animal
el color — the color
el hospital — the hospital
el restaurante — the restaurant
💡 Notice one honest thing: the words ending in -a (familia, música) follow your rule and take la. But cognates that end in a consonant or -e — el animal, el color, el restaurante — don't have an -o or -a to read, so you learn their gender as you meet them. The -o/-a pattern still handles most words; these just aren't the -o/-a kind.
