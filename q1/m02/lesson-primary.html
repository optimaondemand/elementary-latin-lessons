📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why did Rome grow up exactly where it did?
A city's location is never an accident. Rome was built across a cluster of hills, next to a river that connected it to the sea, inside a peninsula shaped like a boot. Today you'll learn the geography that shaped Rome — and two Latin words hiding inside English words you already know.

🗺️ PART 2 · READING: THE CITY ON SEVEN HILLS
Rōma did not start out on flat ground. According to old tradition, the earliest city grew up across seven hills, including the Palatine and the Capitoline — each one called a mōns in Latin. Beside those hills ran the Tiber River, which carried people and goods into the growing city. All of this sat inside Italia, a peninsula shaped like a tall boot reaching into the Mediterranean Sea.
Italia — the boot-shaped peninsula, with Rōma marked in gold

The city of Rome grew up in a very special spot. Here are three things to know about it.

⛰️ Did you know?Rome was built on seven hills. Long ago, families lived on the hilltops and later joined together into one city.
🌊 Did you know?A river called the Tiber flows right through the city. Romans used it for water, for boats, and for trade.
⏳ Did you know?Rome has lasted such a long time that people call it the “Eternal City” — the city that goes on and on.
🔍 PART 2a · WORD DETECTIVE

One more Latin word for today: īnsula, meaning "island." Put it together with paene ("almost"), and you get paene + īnsula — "almost an island" — which became the English word peninsula. That's exactly what Italia is: land that is almost surrounded by water on every side. The word insular (meaning cut off, like an island) also grew from īnsula.
