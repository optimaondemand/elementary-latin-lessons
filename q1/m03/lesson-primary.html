📖 TODAY'S FOCUS
Why do letters still show up where numbers should be?
Ancient Romans didn't write numbers the way we do today. Instead of digits like 1, 2, and 3, they built numbers out of letters: I, V, and X. That system is called Roman numerals, and it never fully disappeared — it still shows up on clocks, in the numbering of the Super Bowl, and inside plenty of books. Today you'll learn to read and say the numbers one through ten the Roman way.

🕰️ PART 2 · READING: NUMBERS THAT NEVER LEFT
Long after Latin stopped being anyone's everyday spoken language, Roman numerals kept showing up in the modern world. Many analog clocks still print their hours using Roman numerals instead of the digits 1 through 12 — and on some of those clocks, the number four is written IIII instead of IV, an old habit among clockmakers that has stuck around for centuries. Each Super Bowl also gets its own Roman numeral instead of a regular number, so a football fan might see a game advertised as Super Bowl LVIII rather than "Super Bowl 58." Publishers still use Roman numerals too, printing them on chapter openers and on the introduction pages of many books.
